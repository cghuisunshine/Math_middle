LCM Denominator Practice

Instructions: Alice, Bob, and Carol each choose a fraction with numerator 1. For each question, find all possible denominators of Carol's fraction.

For each problem, split every LCD into prime-power parts and a power of x. Then solve each part separately.

Note: Alice and Bob can also have multiple possible denominators. The Alice and Bob entries shown in the answer tables are examples that make the listed Carol denominator work.

Question 1

The simplest common denominator of Alice's and Bob's fractions is:

12x2

The simplest common denominator of Bob's and Carol's fractions is:

18x4

The simplest common denominator of Alice's and Carol's fractions is:

36x4

Find all possible denominators of Carol's fraction.

Answer: 9x4 or 18x4

Prime-power check: 12 = 22 · 3, 18 = 2 · 32, and 36 = 22 · 32.

  • x: Alice and Bob only reach x2, but the two LCDs with Carol reach x4. So Carol must have x4.
  • 2: Bob and Carol only reach 21, so Carol can have 20 or 21.
  • 3: The two LCDs with Carol require 32, so Carol must have 32.

Combine: 20 · 32 · x4 = 9x4, or 21 · 32 · x4 = 18x4.

Question 2

The simplest common denominator of Alice's and Bob's fractions is:

20x3

The simplest common denominator of Bob's and Carol's fractions is:

30x5

The simplest common denominator of Alice's and Carol's fractions is:

60x5

Find all possible denominators of Carol's fraction.

Answer: 3x5, 6x5, 15x5, or 30x5

Prime-power check: 20 = 22 · 5, 30 = 2 · 3 · 5, and 60 = 22 · 3 · 5.

  • x: Alice and Bob only reach x3, but the two LCDs with Carol reach x5. So Carol must have x5.
  • 2: Bob and Carol only reach 21, so Carol can have 20 or 21.
  • 3: Alice and Bob have no factor 3 in their LCD, but the two LCDs with Carol do. So Carol must have 31.
  • 5: All three LCDs have 51, so Carol can have 50 or 51.

Combine: 20 or 1 · 31 · 50 or 1 · x5, giving 3x5, 6x5, 15x5, or 30x5.

Question 3

The simplest common denominator of Alice's and Bob's fractions is:

24x4

The simplest common denominator of Bob's and Carol's fractions is:

40x6

The simplest common denominator of Alice's and Carol's fractions is:

120x6

Find all possible denominators of Carol's fraction.

Answer: 5x6, 10x6, 20x6, or 40x6

Prime-power check: 24 = 23 · 3, 40 = 23 · 5, and 120 = 23 · 3 · 5.

  • x: Alice and Bob only reach x4, but the two LCDs with Carol reach x6. So Carol must have x6.
  • 2: All three LCDs require 23. Alice and Bob could supply that, so Carol can have 20, 21, 22, or 23.
  • 3: Bob and Carol have no factor 3 in their LCD, so Carol cannot have a factor 3.
  • 5: Alice and Bob have no factor 5 in their LCD, but the two LCDs with Carol do. So Carol must have 51.

Combine: 20, 1, 2, or 3 · 5 · x6, giving 5x6, 10x6, 20x6, or 40x6.

Question 4

The simplest common denominator of Bob's and Carol's fractions is:

75x5

The simplest common denominator of Alice's and Carol's fractions is:

Find all possible denominators of Carol's fraction.

Answer: 25x5 or 75x5

  • 3: Bob and Carol only reach 31, so Carol can have 30 or 31.

Combine: 30 · 52 · x5 = 25x5, or 31 · 52 · x5 = 75x5.

Helpful Method

Do not treat the coefficient as one large number. First factor each coefficient into primes. Then treat each prime and x as separate maximum problems.

The rule is always:

max(two people's powers) = LCD power

For example, in Question 1 the LCDs are:

LCD power of 2 power of 3 power of x
12x2 22 31 x2
18x4 21 32 x4
36x4 22 32 x4

So Question 1 becomes three smaller questions:

For x: Alice and Bob only reach x2, but the two LCDs involving Carol reach x4. Carol must have x4.

For 2: Bob and Carol together only reach 21, so Carol can have 20 or 21.

For 3: both LCDs involving Carol require 32, so Carol must have 32.

Therefore Carol's denominator is 20 · 32 · x4 or 21 · 32 · x4, which gives 9x4 or 18x4.

Use the same method for every question: split into prime powers, solve each maximum problem, then combine the possible parts at the end.
