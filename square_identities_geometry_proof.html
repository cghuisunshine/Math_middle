Geometry Proofs of Algebraic Identities

The formulas are area statements. Cut squares and rectangles into smaller rectangles, name each area, and then add or subtract the pieces that make the same total region.

1. Building a square with side a + b

(a+b)2 = a2 + 2ab + b2

Area argument

  1. Draw one square whose side length is a + b. Its total area is (a+b)2.
  2. Split each side into lengths a and b.
  3. The four pieces have areas a2, ab, ab, and b2.
  4. Add the pieces: a2 + ab + ab + b2 = a2 + 2ab + b2.

The same square is being measured two ways: as one large square, and as four smaller regions.

2. Removing strips from a square with side a

(a-b)2 = a2 - 2ab + b2

Area argument

  1. Start with a square whose side length is a. Its area is a2.
  2. To leave a square of side a - b, remove two b-wide strips: one on the right and one on the bottom. Each strip has area ab.
  3. The corner overlap was removed twice, because it belongs to both strips.
  4. Add back one b2 corner once: a2 - ab - ab + b2 = a2 - 2ab + b2.

The remaining unremoved square has side length a - b, so its area is also (a - b)2.

3. Rearranging the leftover area after removing b2

a2 - b2 = (a+b)(a-b)

Area argument

  1. Start with a square of side a, then remove a smaller square of side b.
  2. The leftover area is a2 - b2. It is an L-shaped region.
  3. Cut that L-shaped region into two rectangles and rotate the smaller rectangle.
  4. The two pieces form one rectangle whose sides are a + b and a - b.
  5. So the same area is also (a+b)(a-b).

Because rearranging pieces does not change area, the leftover area a2 - b2 equals the rectangle area (a + b)(a - b).

4. Splitting a rectangle with sides a + b and c + d

(a+b)(c+d) = ac + ad + bc + bd

Area argument

  1. Draw one rectangle whose side lengths are a + b and c + d. Its total area is (a+b)(c+d).
  2. Split one side into lengths a and b, and the other side into lengths c and d.
  3. The four smaller rectangles have areas ac, ad, bc, and bd.
  4. Add the pieces: ac + ad + bc + bd.

The same rectangle is measured two ways: once as one large product, and once as the sum of its four smaller rectangular areas.

5. Removing strips from an a by b rectangle

(a-c)(b-d) = ab - bc - ad + cd

1. Subtract bc = c(b-d) + cd

2. Subtract ad = d(a-c) + cd, so cd is hit again

3. Add cd back to cancel the extra subtraction

Area argument

  1. Start with one rectangle whose side lengths are a and b. Its area is ab.
  2. Split the width into a - c and c, and split the height into b - d and d.
  3. The four pieces show ab = (a-c)(b-d) + c(b-d) + d(a-c) + cd.
  4. The right strip is bc = c(b-d) + cd. Subtract it first: ab - bc = (a-c)(b-d) + d(a-c).
  5. The bottom strip is ad = d(a-c) + cd. Subtract it next: ab - bc - ad = (a-c)(b-d) - cd.
  6. The extra -cd appears because the cd corner belongs to both removed rectangles, so subtracting bc and ad removes that corner twice.
  7. Add one cd back: ab - bc - ad + cd = (a-c)(b-d).
  8. Rearrange to get (a-c)(b-d) = ab - bc - ad + cd.

Equivalently, if you compute ab - bc - ad, the cd corner has been removed twice. Adding one cd restores the correct remaining rectangle.

What the geometry proves

When adding lengths A square of side a + b naturally breaks into one a2 square, two matching ab rectangles, and one b2 square.
When subtracting lengths A square of side a - b can be found inside an a2 square by subtracting two ab strips and restoring the b2 overlap.
When subtracting squares Removing a b2 corner from an a2 square leaves pieces that rearrange into a rectangle with side lengths a + b and a - b.
When multiplying two sums A rectangle with side lengths a + b and c + d splits into four rectangles whose areas are ac, ad, bc, and bd.
When subtracting from two sides An a by b rectangle can be trimmed by a bc strip and an ad strip; the shared cd corner is restored once.
