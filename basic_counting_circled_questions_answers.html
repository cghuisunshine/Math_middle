Section 2.2

2.2.1 How many 3-letter words can we make from the letters A, B, C, and D if repetition is allowed and the letter A must appear at least once?

Solution

Count all possible 3-letter words, then subtract the words containing no A.

\[ 4^3-3^3=64-27=37 \]

Answer: \(\boxed{37}\)

2.2.2 One hat contains balls numbered 1 through 15. Another hat contains balls numbered 16 through 25. First choose one hat, then select 3 balls from that hat without replacement. How many different ordered selections are possible?

Solution

From the first hat:

\[ 15\cdot14\cdot13=2730 \]

From the second hat:

\[ 10\cdot9\cdot8=720 \]
\[ 2730+720=3450 \]

Answer: \(\boxed{3450}\)

2.2.3 How many directed paths are there from A to H in Figure 2.9 if travel is allowed only in the direction of the arrows?

Solution

First count the paths from A to D.

\[ (2\cdot1)+(2\cdot3)=2+6=8 \]

Then count the paths from D to H.

\[ (1\cdot1)+(2\cdot3)+(2\cdot1)=1+6+2=9 \]

Every path from A to H passes through D, so multiply:

\[ 8\cdot9=72 \]

Answer: \(\boxed{72}\)

2.2.5 How many different rectangles can be formed by connecting four dots in a \(4\times4\) square array, with the sides of each rectangle parallel to the sides of the grid?

Solution

Choose two of the four rows and two of the four columns.

\[ \binom{4}{2}\binom{4}{2}=6\cdot6=36 \]

Squares are included because every square is also a rectangle.

Answer: \(\boxed{36}\)

Section 2.3 — Complementary Counting

Problem 2.6 How many three-digit numbers are not multiples of 7?

Solution

There are \(900\) three-digit numbers.

The first three-digit multiple of 7 is \(105=15\cdot7\), and the last is \(994=142\cdot7\).

\[ 142-15+1=128 \]

Therefore:

\[ 900-128=772 \]

Answer: \(\boxed{772}\)

Problem 2.7 The Smith family has 4 sons and 3 daughters. In how many ways can they be seated in a row of 7 chairs so that at least 2 boys sit next to each other?

Solution

Count all arrangements, then subtract arrangements in which no two boys are adjacent.

\[ 7!=5040 \]

To keep all four boys separated by the three girls, the arrangement must have the pattern

\[ BGBGBGB. \]

The boys can be arranged in \(4!\) ways and the girls in \(3!\) ways.

\[ 4!\cdot3!=144 \]
\[ 5040-144=4896 \]

Answer: \(\boxed{4896}\)

2.3.1 How many 4-letter words containing at least one vowel can be constructed from A, B, C, D, and E, where A and E are vowels?

Solution

Count all words, then subtract those containing no vowel.

\[ 5^4-3^4=625-81=544 \]

Answer: \(\boxed{544}\)

2.3.2 How many 5-digit numbers contain at least one zero?

Solution

There are \(90{,}000\) five-digit numbers in total.

A five-digit number with no zero has 9 choices for each digit:

\[ 9^5=59049 \]
\[ 90000-59049=30951 \]

Answer: \(\boxed{30951}\)

2.3.3 A person has 6 shirts, 6 pairs of pants, and 6 hats. Each item is available in the same 6 colours, one item of each colour. If an outfit cannot have all three items in the same colour, how many outfits are possible?

Solution

There are \(6\) choices for each of the three items.

\[ 6\cdot6\cdot6=216 \]

There are 6 forbidden outfits in which all three items have the same colour.

\[ 216-6=210 \]

Answer: \(\boxed{210}\)

2.3.4 In how many ways can 7 people be seated in a row of 7 chairs if Wilma and Paul refuse to sit next to each other?

Solution

Start with all arrangements:

\[ 7!=5040 \]

When Wilma and Paul sit together, treat them as one block. The six objects can be arranged in \(6!\) ways, and the two people inside the block can switch places.

\[ 2\cdot6!=1440 \]
\[ 5040-1440=3600 \]

Answer: \(\boxed{3600}\)
