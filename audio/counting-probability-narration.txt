Lesson 5

Partial Permutations

Choose and arrange only part of a group.

Key idea

Proof

Worked example

A club has 10 members. How many ways can it choose a president, vice-president, and treasurer?

Order matters because the jobs are different.

Common mistake

Do not use combinations here. Choosing Alice as president and Ben as treasurer is different from choosing Ben as president and Alice as treasurer.

Practice

How many 3-person officer teams can be formed from 12 people if the offices are president, secretary, and treasurer?

Lesson 6

Casework

Split a problem into non-overlapping cases, count each case, then add.

Key idea

Casework works when every valid object belongs to exactly one case. Then:

Why it works

If cases are exclusive, no object is counted twice. If cases are complete, no object is missed. Therefore adding all case counts gives the total.

Worked example

How many 3-digit numbers have exactly one zero?

Common mistake

Do not allow zero in the hundreds place. A 3-digit number cannot begin with 0.

Practice

How many 3-digit numbers have exactly two even digits?

Lesson 7

Complementary Counting

Count what you do not want, then subtract from the total.

Key idea

This is often useful for “not,” “at least one,” or “no restrictions except...” problems.

Why it works

Worked example

How many 3-digit numbers are not multiples of 7?

Common mistake

Make sure the total universe is clear. Here the universe is all 3-digit numbers, not all positive integers.

Practice

How many 4-digit PINs contain at least one 7? Digits may repeat, and a PIN may begin with 0.

Final Advice

Counting is not about memorizing many formulas. It is about building a clean counting process, checking whether choices are independent, separating cases carefully, and correcting overcounts honestly.
