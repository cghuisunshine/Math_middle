1.1 Numbers

Review whole numbers, negatives, fractions, decimals, and irrational numbers so later algebra statements have precise meaning.

Key idea: A strong algebra student can tell whole numbers, integers, rationals, and irrationals apart and knows when each one appears.
Quick example: 7 is a whole number, -3 is an integer, 2/5 is rational, and √2 is irrational.

1.2 Order of Operations

Learn the standard order for evaluating expressions so every reader interprets the same string of symbols in the same way.

Key idea: Multiply and divide before adding and subtracting unless parentheses change the order.
Quick example: 2 + 3 × 4 = 2 + 12 = 14, not 20.
Study move: Read the rule, redo the example without looking, and then invent one similar problem of your own.
Common trap: Reading passively without testing whether you can reproduce the method on a fresh example.

1.3 When Does Order Matter?

Compare operations such as addition and multiplication with subtraction and division, where reversing order can change the answer.

Key idea: Addition and multiplication are commutative, but subtraction and division usually are not.
Quick example: 5 - 2 = 3, but 2 - 5 = -3, so order clearly matters.
Study move: Read the rule, redo the example without looking, and then invent one similar problem of your own.
Common trap: Reading passively without testing whether you can reproduce the method on a fresh example.
Mini practice

1.4 Distribution and Factoring

Use the distributive law to expand products and reverse the process to reveal common structure by factoring.

Key idea: Distribution expands a product; factoring reverses that move to expose common structure.
Quick example: 3(x + 4) = 3x + 12, and 3x + 12 = 3(x + 4).
Study move: Before expanding anything, pause and ask whether the expression matches a known pattern such as a square, a difference of squares, or a quadratic in disguise.
Common trap: Expanding too early and destroying a pattern that would have made the problem much shorter.
Mini practice

Check: You should get 5a - 10 and 5(2y + 3).

1.5 Equations

Introduce equations as balance statements about unknown numbers and solve them with reversible steps.

Key idea: An equation is a balance statement, so whatever you do to one side must also be done to the other.
Quick example: x + 5 = 12 leads to x = 7 after subtracting 5 from both sides.
Study move: Rewrite first, isolate second, and always substitute your answer back into the original equation.
Common trap: Changing only one side of the equation or forgetting that squaring can introduce extra solutions.
Mini practice

Check: Add 4 first, then divide by 3.
